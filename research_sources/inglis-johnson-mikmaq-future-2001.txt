    Before examining the structure of the A l Future a brief outline of the
system of modality is needed. Modality in Mi'kmaq is characterized by a
set of suffixes, primarily evidentials, which mark "...the ways in which
ordinary people ... naturally regard the source and reliability of their
knowledge" (Chafe and Nichols 1986:vii). The main evidential suffixes in
Mi'kmaq are attestive -p(ri), suppositive -s(n) and inferential -s(i)p(n).

Mi'kmaq meaning 'the Allies'. Mi'kmaq is the spelling using the Smith-Francis orthogra-
phy which is used throughout this paper (see Hewson and Francis 1990:ii-iii). In this
orthography schwa is represented by a barred /i/ and vowel length is represented by an

THE MI'KMAQ Al FUTURE: AN ANALYSIS
    There are four observations which can be made about the Mi'kmaq
Al Future which will allow us to draw some conclusions concerning its
cognitive framework as well as its historical origins:

(1) The Al Future developed from a Proto-Algonquian (PA) type II verb form
    (discussed below) which originally had a full set of personal prefixes and
    suffixes.
(2) The endings of the Al Future contain the evidential suffixes -s(n) l-s(i)p(n
    which mark for suppositive / inferential knowledge respectively.
(3) The -te(k) element found in the endings of the Al Future is a
    grammaticalized form of the II Independent neutral of the verb 'to be', etek.
(4) The Al Future forms are characterized by an unchanged or reduced stem
    which marks for irrealis modality.

    There is evidence (Inglis 1998) showing that as personal prefixes and
suffixes are dropped from the Mi'kmaq language, with respect to posses-
sion, it is the 12 and 13 personal suffixes, -inu and -inen, which appear to
be the last to go. In a study done on language death in the Cape Breton
Mi'kmaq communities of Eskasoni and Wagmatcook, senior Mi'kmaq
university students at the University College of Cape Breton, found that
among many younger Mi'kmaq speakers all the personal prefixes and suf-
fixes had been dropped for possessed nouns, except for the 12 and 13 suf-
fixes, -inu and -inen.
    The same type of language change as we have witnessed over the last
decade with respect to the dropping of personal suffixes on possessed
nouns could have taken place in terms of the Al Future if w e surmise that
originally the Future was a PA Type II verb form which exhibited both

THE MI'KMAQ Al FUTURE CONTAINS EVIDENTIALS
     T h e A l Future forms contain the evidential suffixes, -s(n), and
-s(i)p(n). U s e of suppositive/inferential evidential suffixes in a verb form
notionally expressing hypothetical potential actions (actions not yet done)
makes cognitive sense for one can only 'suppose' or 'infer' about 'the
future'.
     Evidentials in M i ' k m a q are placed on top of neutral forms in most
instances; they are non-paradigmatic forms. For example sentence (2)
gives the II Independent neutral form, tekpa'q, meaning 'it is cold.' This
contrasts with sentence (3) which show the nearly identical sentence but
with the suppositive evidential -s being suffixed to the Independent neu-
tral form of (2):

(2) Independent neutral

    Tekpa 'qas.

    -tek is the reduced variant of the II Independent neutral of the verb 'to
be', etek. Reducing the verb etek and grammaticalizing the reduced form
such that it functions as an existential modal suffix captures the irrealis
sense that a future form needs to convey. T h e form, -tek, of the A l Future
appears to be a suffix created by grammaticalization for the purpose of
expressing existentialism - to exist. T h e infinitive of etek is eymik. A s
noted by H e w s o n and Francis (1990:123)
    Maillard gives in Micmac eymik ... [but it] does not mean to be some-

    am here, na'te'l eymn, thou art there ...

potential hypothetical situation or existential space sits well with a
'Future'. A s noted by B y b e e (1985:168)

THE MI'KMAQ Al FUTURE HAS A REDUCED STEM
      There are two types of modality in Mi'kmaq: primary and secondary.
Primary modality is realized thorough the used of full and reduced stems
which mark realis and irrealis modality respectively. Secondary modality
is marked by various evidential suffixes. In the Al Future forms both
types of modality are present: reduced stems of Future verbs mark for
irrealis modality while the existential suffix -tek and the suppositive/infer-
ential evidentials -s(n) l-s(i)p(n), mark for supposed hypothetical possi-
bleness - a future.


CONCLUSION

      In conclusion it appears that the Mi'kmaq Al Future forms could be
remnants of a PA Type II verb which originally had personal prefixes and
suffixes. One way to achieve the notion of future or unrealized/unexperi-
enced events in such a highly modal language would be to grammaticalize
the reduced form of the II Independent neutral of the verb 'to be', -tek,
thus creating an existential suffix and to couple that notion of existential-
ism with the modality of the suppositive/inferential evidentials. Then, by
reducing the stem of the verb the concept of irrealis is heightened giving
the sense of a remote or distance hypothetical event which one may sup-
pose of or infer about - i.e. a future. In summary w e conclude that to build
a future form in Mi'kmaq, modal suffixes (existential and evidential) are
used on an irrealis stem creating a Future form but, not a future tense.

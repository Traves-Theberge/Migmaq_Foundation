# Mui'n, Mui'n, Wen Nemi't?
## *Bear, Bear, Who Do You See?*

A bilingual Mi'kmaq–English children's book using an original chained call-and-response
structure — a generic, uncopyrightable question-and-answer pattern common to many early
readers. All text, wording, animal choices, and ordering here are original to this project;
none of it is derived from any copyrighted work. Built entirely from vocabulary and sentence
frames attested in this project's corpus.

> **Status: draft — requires review by a fluent Mi'kmaq speaker before use with children.**
> Text is assembled from historical dictionary sources (Rand, Clark, Pacifique) and the
> project grammar cheatsheet. Orthography follows the project's transliteration conventions.
>
> **Specific points for speaker review:**
> 1. Direct address of an animal by its plain noun (*Mui'n, mui'n, …*): the cheatsheet only
>    documents special vocative forms for kinship terms (§3.2a); whether plain-noun address
>    is natural here is undocumented.
> 2. Whether the question would more naturally carry the overt pronoun (*Wen gi'l nemi't?*)
>    or a demonstrative, as in the attested *Gi's gi'l nemi't sasqatu?*
> 3. The finale's run of ten first-person clauses (see note on page 11).
> 4. Word order in the question frame: `wen` follows the vocative address (*[X], [X],*) rather than
>    being the literal first word of the whole utterance. The cheatsheet's rule is that
>    *sentence-initial* `wen` reads as "who?", while `wen` appearing later can shift to an
>    indefinite "someone/anyone" reading — and separately, the Wiki states a wh-word placed in the
>    *middle* of a sentence is generally rejected (echo questions are not possible; `*i-nemis'p tami
>    tett?` is rejected precisely because the question word comes after other clause material).
>    This book's frame is **not** that configuration, though: there is a directly attested example
>    of a vocative address followed by a comma and then a clause with its own ordinary word order —
>    *tus, pasi* "Daughter, sit!" (§3.2a) — where the imperative `pasi` simply starts its clause
>    normally, unaffected by the vocative before it. That supports treating `[X], [X],` as
>    extra-clausal (outside the sentence proper), so `wen` remains effectively clause-initial and
>    should keep its "who?" reading rather than sliding into the mid-sentence-rejected or
>    later-indefinite pattern. The supporting example is an imperative, not a question, so this
>    should still be confirmed with a speaker rather than treated as fully settled.

## The sentence frames

| Mi'kmaq | English | Attestation |
|---|---|---|
| `[X], [X], wen nemi't?` | X, X, who do you see? | `nemi't` "you see him/her" is the attested 2→3 present-indicative VTA ending (`-(V)'t`, cheatsheet §10.1, confirmed against the wiki's own raw source table), and is directly attested whole-word in the corpus example *Gi's gi'l nemi't sasqatu?* "Have you seen a squirrel?" (Clark p. 183, `reference_materials/Clark/markdown/page-0183.md`); `wen` "who/anyone" as object of *nemi-* attested in *nemij wen, tlimitis* (cheatsheet) |
| `Nemi'g [Y].` | I see a Y. | `nemi'g epit` "I see the woman" (cheatsheet, VTA animate-object row) |

**Why this frame is grammatically safe** (verified against the cheatsheet's VTA present
indicative subject-by-object matrix, §10.1):

- **`nemi'g`** = *nemi-* + `-(V)'g`, the attested 1→3 ending ("I see him/her").
- **`nemi't`** = *nemi-* + `-(V)'t`, the attested 2→3 ending ("you see him/her") — confirmed
  directly against the wiki's raw source table (subject row 2, object column 3 = `-(V)'t`) and,
  better still, attested as a whole word in the real corpus question *Gi's gi'l nemi't sasqatu?*
  "Have you seen a squirrel?" (Clark p. 183) — a genuine 2sg-subject, 3rd-person-object question
  using this exact form. (An earlier draft of this note also cited the past-tense form `nemi't'p`
  as corroboration; that citation was wrong and has been removed — the wiki glosses `gil nemit-'p`
  as "you see.**2>1**-PST" = "you saw **me**", not "you saw him/her". The past-tense theme sign
  does not necessarily track the same subject/object assignment as its present-tense counterpart,
  so it cannot be used as corroboration here; the present-tense table and the corpus question above
  are the only evidence this claim rests on.)
- All the animals are animate nouns, so the verb is the VTA stem *nemi-* (not VTI *nemitu-*,
  which is for inanimate objects — that is why the book does not use *Goqwei nemitu'n?*
  "what do you see?").
- Question morphology: per §13, information questions ordinarily put the question word (`wen`)
  clause-initial, and Mi'kmaq has no who/whom distinction. This book's frame puts `wen` after a
  vocative address instead of literally first in the clause — see review point 4 above for why
  that specific configuration is not directly attested and needs a speaker check. The evidential
  question suffixes (`nemi's'p?` etc.) belong to **past** questions only (§13.2, missing-details
  "only the indirect evidential appears in past questions"); present-tense questions carry no
  extra marking, which is why this frame needs no such suffix regardless of the word-order question.
- **Word order within the clause is confirmed correct**: the question word must precede the verb,
  not follow it. This is directly attested by a rejected counterexample in the cheatsheet's echo-question
  material — `*i-nemis'p tami tett?` (verb, then question word) is explicitly rejected, while
  `tami tett i-nemis'p?` (question word, then verb) is the accepted form (§13.1a). The book's frame,
  *wen nemi't?* (question word `wen`, then verb `nemi't`), follows the accepted question-word-first
  order, not the rejected verb-first order. The answer frame, *Nemi'g [Y]* (verb, then object), is
  separately attested as ordinary VO order for this stem (*nemi'g epit* "I see the woman", §1) —
  Mi'kmaq word order is otherwise flexible/discourse-driven (§12), so this VO order is not the only
  grammatical option, but it is a genuinely attested one, not an invented default.
- Because the question is 2nd person ("you see") and the answer is 1st person ("I see"), no
  clause ever has two 3rd persons, so **obviative marking never applies** — the plain noun form
  is correct throughout. (Compare *lentug nemiatl mui'nal* "the deer sees the bear", where the
  3→4 ending `-atl` and obviative `-al` on the noun are required.)
- Since `nemi't` is unambiguously the 2→3 cell, *Wen nemi't?* can only be read with *wen* as
  object ("who do you see?"), never "who sees him?" (which would need 3→4 *nemiatl*).

The original book's "…looking at me" clause is deliberately dropped: the simple frame is
attested verbatim in the corpus; a participial "looking at me" would require unattested
morphology.

*Illustration: the child in the middle of a spread with all ten animals gathered around.*

**Layout note:** this page also serves as the back cover. Its text block (both the
question and the ten-sentence answer) should stay **center-aligned** on the page, not
left- or block-justified, when laid out there.

*(Two deliberate choices here. Each `nemi'g X` is set as its own sentence so every clause
contains exactly one third person, respecting the discourse-obviation "spotlight" rule
(cheatsheet §10.2: only one third person is proximate within a stretch of discourse).
And the finale keeps singular objects even though the 1sg→3pl ending IS attested
(`-(V)'gig`, so *nemi'gig* "I see them"): plural noun forms are only attested for some of
the animals — mui'naq, apli'gmujg, sasqatu'g, gopgwejg, plamu'g — while jipji'j, antawe's,
gopit, lentug, and tia'm have no attested plural in the project corpus, and the cheatsheet's
plural allomorphy (`-g`/`-ug`/`-ig`/`-oq`/`-q`) is too variable to generate them safely.)*

## Note for parents and teachers

Mi'kmaq verbs change with **who is seeing** and **what kind of thing is seen**:

- **nemi'g** — "I see him/her" — used for *animate* things (people and animals).
- **nemi't** — "you see him/her" — the same verb, with a *you* ending.
- **nemitu** — "I see it" — a different ending used for *inanimate* things (like a table).

Every animal in this book is grammatically **animate**, so the whole book runs on one verb
stem, *nemi-*, in just two forms — the question form and the answer form. Each page asks
the question with a new animal's name and answers it with the next one, so a child hears
every animal's name twice: once being asked, once being seen.
